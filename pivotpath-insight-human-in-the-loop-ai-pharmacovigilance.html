Human-in-the-loop AI in pharmacovigilance: what good looks like

AI can absorb the routine load in pharmacovigilance — but a human must stay accountable for every safety decision. Here is what a defensible operating model looks like.

A single missed adverse event can become a regulatory finding, a patient-safety risk, and a reputational problem all at once. That is why the promise of AI in pharmacovigilance — faster case intake, cleaner triage, earlier signals — sits alongside a hard question every safety leader is now asked in audits: who is accountable when the model is wrong?

The pressure is real, and so is the risk

Case volumes keep climbing while regulatory expectations tighten. Manual case processing does not scale linearly, and the temptation is to hand the whole pipeline to automation. But pharmacovigilance is a regulated safety function, not a back-office workflow. An AI that silently mislabels a serious case, or drops a signal, does not just create rework — it creates liability. The failure mode is quiet, and quiet failures are the ones that surface late.

What good looks like

The standard the buyer is measured against is traceability. Under 21 CFR Part 11 and EU Annex 11, regulated records and the decisions behind them must be attributable, legible, and auditable — every action logged and timestamped. Regulators are increasingly explicit that AI in a GxP context is expected to keep a human accountable for the decision; the model informs, it does not decide.

That distinction is the whole game. Automation is welcome for the repetitive, high-volume, low-judgement work. Accountability stays with a named, qualified person for anything that touches a safety conclusion.

The Pivot Path angle

We don’t just advise on this operating model — we run it. Our pharmacovigilance work draws a clear line between what runs hands-off and what stays human-in-the-loop:

  • Automated: case intake, de-duplication, data extraction, and first-pass triage — the routine load AI handles well and consistently.
  • Human-owned: causality assessment, seriousness and expectedness determinations, and every signal decision — reviewed and signed by a qualified safety professional.

The principle we hold to across every AI-enabled deployment is simple: AI outputs are recommendations, never decisions. That is what keeps the system defensible when an auditor asks how a conclusion was reached — the answer is a person, supported by a logged, reviewable trail, not a black box.

You can see how this shows up in our patient-safety operations, and in NovaVigil, our pharmacovigilance platform built around the same human-in-the-loop discipline.

FAQ

Can AI process adverse event cases end to end?

It can process the routine, structured parts of the workflow — intake, de-duplication, data extraction, first-pass triage. Safety conclusions such as causality and seriousness stay with a qualified reviewer, under change control and full audit trail.

Is using AI in pharmacovigilance compliant with 21 CFR Part 11?

Yes, when it is deployed correctly. Part 11 and EU Annex 11 require attributable, auditable records and decisions. Keeping a human accountable for safety determinations, with every action logged, is what makes an AI-enabled workflow defensible in audit.

How do you stop AI from missing a signal?

By design: the model triages and surfaces, but signal decisions are made and documented by people. The value of automation is that it frees expert time to focus on exactly those judgement calls, rather than on manual data handling.
